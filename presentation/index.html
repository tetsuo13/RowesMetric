  • Identifying "suspicious" behavior in video surveillance.
  • Rank agents for deceptiveness using Rowe's exposure metric.
  • Noticeability and suspiciousness are two of the most important metrics in measuring the exposure of a deceptive agent.

Background

  • Identifying suspicious or deceptive people (agents) in video.
  • A difficult problem, made easier through analytics.


Huge applications in the video surveillance field:
Grocery stores - Malls - Airports

Rowe's Metric

Metric for "exposure" of a deceptive agent:

\[ E = (1/T)\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1} s(t) \sum_{i = 0}^{N} v(i, t) n(i, t) \]
\[ E = (1/T)\sum_{t = 0}^{T - 1} \color{Orange}{s(t)} \sum_{i = 0}^{N} v(i, t) \color{Orange}{n(i, t)} \]

Noticeability

\[ n(i, t) = \frac{k_3}{k_3 + p(x,y)} \]

Probability \(p(x,y)\) in region


Suspiciousness

\[ s(t) = \frac{k_1}{k_1 + p(x,y) q(s,h)} \]

Distribution \(q(s,h)\) of speed and heading

Design

  • Population of 7 billion, sample size of 9.
  • Microsoft Kinect sensor device for motion capture.

Future work

  • Ideal number of regions to split bounding box into should be derived from data instead of using a constant.
  • False positives can occur — janitor, security guard, children.
  • Use metadata to calculate \(k_1\) and \(k_3\).
    \(n(i, t) = \frac{k_3}{k_3 + p(x,y)}\) \(s(t) = \frac{k_1}{k_1 + p(x,y) q(s,h)}\)
